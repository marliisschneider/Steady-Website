Heartburn and reflux: the food fixes that actually work.

Before you resign yourself to a daily antacid, know this: most everyday heartburn responds to a handful of food and habit changes — how much you eat, when you eat it, what your triggers are, and what you do right after. Reflux is often mechanical, which means it's often fixable at the table.

What reflux actually is

At the top of your stomach is a ring of muscle — a valve — that's supposed to keep stomach contents down where they belong. Reflux is what happens when that valve relaxes or gets overwhelmed and acid escapes upward into the esophagus, which isn't built to handle it. That burning behind your breastbone is the result. Notice the framing: it's largely about pressure and mechanics, not simply "too much acid" — which is why how and when you eat matters as much as what.

The usual triggers

Two categories drive most everyday reflux. First, how you eat: large meals, eating late, and lying down or slouching right after. Second, trigger foods — commonly fried and fatty foods, spicy dishes, citrus, tomato, chocolate, coffee, alcohol, and carbonated drinks. The catch is that triggers are individual: your list isn't your friend's. That's why chasing a food diary beats blanket-cutting everything.

The pressure angle: weight and posture

Anything that increases pressure on your stomach pushes acid upward. Extra belly weight does it around the clock; a big meal does it acutely; lying down removes gravity's help entirely. This is why the same person can be fine with a light early dinner and miserable after a late, heavy one — the food might be similar, but the pressure and timing aren't.

How to calm reflux with food and habits

  • Eat smaller meals. A stuffed stomach is a pressurized stomach. Smaller, more moderate meals reflux far less than large ones.
  • Don't lie down for 2–3 hours after eating. The single biggest lever for nighttime reflux. Finish dinner earlier and stay upright.
  • Find your triggers, don't guess them. Keep a one-week food-and-symptom note. Cut what actually flares you, and keep the foods that don't — no need to give up tomatoes if tomatoes aren't your problem.
  • Address belly weight if it applies. Even modest loss reduces the constant upward pressure — often more effective than any single food change.
  • Elevate the head of your bed. Raising the head a few inches (or a wedge pillow) uses gravity to keep acid down overnight. Stacking pillows under your head alone doesn't do it.
  • Ease off the late-night and the obvious irritants. Big late meals, excess alcohol and coffee, and carbonation are the common culprits worth trimming first.
Start here tonight

Do two things: make dinner smaller, and finish it at least three hours before bed. For a lot of people, that timing change alone quiets most of the nighttime burn — before touching any food on the "trigger" list.

An important line: this is for mild, occasional heartburn. If reflux happens twice a week or more, persists despite these changes, or comes with trouble swallowing, unexplained weight loss, or symptoms that wake you — see a doctor. Chronic reflux can damage the esophagus over time, and it shouldn't be self-managed indefinitely with over-the-counter antacids. Food and habits are the sensible first step, not a substitute for care when symptoms are frequent or severe.
