The root causes · Dairy

Dairy: friend, not the enemy.

Cutting dairy is one of the most common "clean eating" moves there is. I did it for years, convinced it was behind my inflammation. It wasn't — and adding it back was part of what finally fixed me.

Why I cut it (and why that was a mistake)

Somewhere along the way I'd absorbed the idea that dairy causes inflammation, so I cut it completely — no milk, no butter, no cheese. I was sure I was doing something healthy. My joint pain and bloating didn't budge. When I finally rebuilt my diet around whole foods, I put full-fat dairy back in alongside cutting seed oils and processed food. Nothing got worse. Everything got better. The dairy was never the problem.

The pattern to notice

When people cut dairy and feel better, they usually cut a lot of other things at the same time — the sugary flavored yogurt, the processed cheese on the fast-food burger, the ice cream. It's easy to blame the milk for what the junk around it was doing.

What real dairy actually gives you

Whole, minimally processed dairy is one of the most nutrient-dense foods available:

  • High-quality protein. Milk, yogurt, and cheese are complete proteins that help you hit the targets most people miss.
  • Calcium and vitamin K2. A hard combination to get elsewhere — and K2 helps direct calcium into your bones rather than your arteries.
  • Fat-soluble vitamins. Real butter and full-fat dairy carry vitamins A and D, especially from grass-fed sources.
  • Gut-friendly ferments. Plain yogurt and kefir deliver live cultures that support the gut — the opposite of inflammatory.

Full-fat, not low-fat

Low-fat dairy often has the fat stripped out and sugar added back to compensate — which is exactly backwards. The fat in dairy is what keeps you full and helps you absorb those fat-soluble vitamins. Choose full-fat milk, real butter (grass-fed if you can), plain full-fat yogurt, and real cheese. Skip the sweetened, flavored, and heavily processed versions.

Quality checklist

Full-fat over low-fat · plain over flavored · real cheese over processed slices · grass-fed where you can · and read the label — "dairy" desserts are often just sugar.

Who genuinely should be careful

This isn't a blanket "everyone should eat dairy." Some people truly shouldn't:

  • A real milk allergy — an immune reaction that needs a doctor's guidance, not guesswork.
  • Lactose intolerance — though many people who react to a glass of milk do fine with hard cheese and fermented yogurt, where the lactose is largely gone.
  • A clear, tested individual reaction — if you've properly ruled dairy in as a trigger, honor that.

For most people, though, whole dairy is a traditional, nourishing food — not something to fear. If you're unsure whether it's a problem for you, test it properly instead of cutting it "just in case."

This is general education, not medical advice. If you suspect a dairy allergy or intolerance, work with a qualified healthcare provider.
