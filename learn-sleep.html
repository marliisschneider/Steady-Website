The Steady Library · Sleep

You're not a bad sleeper. You might have a food problem.

You fall asleep fine, then snap awake at 3am — wired, heart going, no reason. It's rarely random. More often, it's your blood sugar, your last drink, or your afternoon coffee. The good news: sleep is built during the day, which means it's fixable at the table.

Your night is built during your day

We treat sleep as something that starts at bedtime — dim the lights, put the phone down, hope for the best. But by the time your head hits the pillow, most of the work is already done or undone. The coffee at 3pm, the wine with dinner, the carb-heavy meal eaten late, the protein you skipped — those are the real levers. Sleep hygiene helps at the margins. What you ate and drank all day decides the game.

The 3am wake-up is often a blood sugar crash

Here's the mechanism most people never hear. If your dinner spiked your blood sugar — a big plate of pasta, dessert, a couple of drinks — you get a matching crash a few hours later, often in the early morning. When blood sugar dips too low overnight, your body doesn't just wait politely. It releases cortisol and adrenaline to pull stored sugar back into your blood. Those are alarm hormones. They're also exactly what wakes you at 3am feeling alert and slightly anxious for no reason you can name.

Not every 3am wake-up is blood sugar — stress, a full bladder, and simply getting older all play a part. But if you wake up hungry, or a few bites of something quiets you back to sleep, that's a strong tell that glucose is involved.

The four things quietly wrecking your sleep

  • Caffeine you drank hours ago. Caffeine has a half-life of roughly 5–6 hours, meaning a 3pm coffee still has half its dose circulating at 8 or 9pm. It doesn't have to keep you from falling asleep to flatten the deep, restorative stages once you're under.
  • The nightcap. Alcohol makes you fall asleep faster, which is exactly why it fools people. But as it wears off in the second half of the night, it fragments sleep and suppresses REM — the dreaming, memory-consolidating stage. A glass of wine can be the reason you're technically in bed eight hours and still exhausted.
  • A late, heavy, or sugary dinner. Eating a large meal right before bed means your body is digesting when it should be winding down, and a high-glucose dinner sets up the overnight crash above. Timing and composition both matter.
  • Under-eating during the day. Skipping meals or barely eating until dinner leaves you either ravenous and over-eating at night, or genuinely under-fueled — both disrupt the steady overnight glucose you need to stay asleep.

How to eat and drink for better sleep

None of this is about a perfect bedtime routine. It's a handful of daytime choices with an outsized effect on the night.

  • Put a cutoff on caffeine. Aim to have your last coffee by early afternoon — for most people, around 2pm. If you're sensitive, earlier. This one change alone deepens sleep for a lot of people without touching how much they drink in the morning.
  • Build a balanced dinner, not a carb bomb. Protein, vegetables, and some fat, with carbs as the side rather than the whole plate. Same sequencing that flattens a daytime spike keeps your blood sugar steady while you sleep.
  • If you wake at 3am, try a small balanced snack before bed. Counterintuitive, but for blood-sugar wake-ups, a few bites of protein and fat at bedtime — a spoon of nut butter, a bit of cheese, some Greek yogurt — can hold your glucose steady through the night. Test it and see.
  • Treat alcohol as an evening trade-off. You don't have to quit. But finish earlier, keep it moderate, and add a glass of water between drinks. The closer to bedtime and the larger the amount, the more it costs your REM.
  • Don't go to bed starving or stuffed. Both wreck sleep. Eat enough during the day that dinner isn't a binge, and finish eating a couple of hours before bed when you can.
  • Get light and protein early. Morning daylight and a protein-forward breakfast help set your circadian clock, which is what makes you sleepy at the right time that night. Sleep is a 24-hour system, not a bedtime event.
Start here tonight

Pick one: move your last coffee to before 2pm, or make tonight's dinner protein-and-vegetables-first with carbs as the side. Just one, for a few nights. Notice whether 3am gets quieter.

If you've genuinely fixed the food and drink and still can't sleep — especially if you snore heavily, wake gasping, or feel unrefreshed no matter what — that's worth a conversation with your doctor. Conditions like sleep apnea are common, under-diagnosed, and not something you can eat your way out of.
