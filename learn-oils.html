The playbook · Fats

The healthiest oils to cook with.

The fat you cook in is one of the highest-leverage choices in your kitchen — and most "healthy" cooking oils are exactly the ones to avoid. Here's what to use for which heat, and what to ditch.

The one rule that decides it: stability

The healthiest oils to cook with are the ones that stay stable when heated. When a fat is heated past its limit, it oxidizes — producing compounds that drive inflammation. Saturated and monounsaturated fats (butter, ghee, olive oil, avocado, coconut) are stable and handle heat well. The industrial seed oils, high in fragile polyunsaturated fat, oxidize easily — which is the whole problem with them.

The good ones — and what each is best for

Oil / fatBest forWhy it works
ButterEggs, low-medium heat, flavorStable saturated fat; carries fat-soluble vitamins
Ghee (clarified butter)Medium-high heat, sautéingHigher smoke point than butter; very stable

Avocado oilHigh-heat searing, roastingMostly monounsaturated; high smoke point
Coconut oilMedium heat, bakingSaturated and heat-stable; neutral-to-sweet flavor
Tallow / duck fatRoasting, frying at homeTraditional, stable animal fats
A note on olive oil and heat

Good extra-virgin olive oil is more heat-tolerant than the internet claims and is fine for most home cooking. For a screaming-hot sear, reach for avocado oil, ghee, or tallow instead.

The ones to ditch

These are the fragile, industrial seed oils — cheap, heavily processed, and prone to oxidizing. They're in most packaged and restaurant food, which is why cooking at home is such a lever:

  • Canola (rapeseed) oil
  • Soybean oil and generic "vegetable oil"
  • Sunflower, safflower, corn, cottonseed, grapeseed oil
  • Margarine and anything "partially hydrogenated"

Swap them for the stable fats above and you remove one of the biggest hidden drivers of inflammation from your diet. Why seed oils matter →

Your kitchen, sorted

Keep three fats on the counter: butter or ghee for everyday cooking, extra-virgin olive oil for dressings and low heat, and avocado oil for high heat. That covers everything — no seed oil required.

The bottom line

"Vegetable oil" sounds wholesome and butter sounds indulgent, but it's backwards. The healthiest oils to cook with are the traditional, stable fats humans have used for centuries — and the ones to ditch are the industrial oils we only started eating in the last several decades.
