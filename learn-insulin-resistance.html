Insulin resistance: the signs everyone misses.

Insulin resistance is one of the most common — and most overlooked — reasons capable people feel tired, foggy, and stuck. It builds quietly for years before a doctor ever mentions it. Here's what it is, the symptoms to watch for, and what actually helps.

Important

This article helps you recognize the pattern — it can't diagnose you. Insulin resistance and prediabetes are confirmed with bloodwork. Use this to have a better conversation with your doctor and to request the right labs.

What insulin resistance actually is

Insulin is the hormone that moves sugar out of your blood and into your cells for energy. When you spike your blood sugar over and over — meal after meal, year after year — your cells gradually stop responding to insulin's knock. So your body makes more insulin to force the door. That's insulin resistance: high insulin, cells that ignore it, and a system working overtime. Left unchecked, it's the road toward prediabetes and type 2 diabetes.

The signs everyone misses

Because it develops slowly, the symptoms get written off as "just getting older" or "being busy." Watch for a cluster of these:

  • Energy crashes after meals — especially carb-heavy ones.
  • Constant hunger and sugar cravings, even after eating.
  • Stubborn weight around the middle that won't budge with the usual efforts.
  • Brain fog and afternoon fatigue.
  • Feeling shaky, irritable, or "hangry" if a meal is late.
  • Skin tags or dark, velvety patches on the neck or armpits (a recognized sign worth asking your doctor about).
  • Bloodwork flags: high triglycerides, low HDL, rising fasting glucose or A1c.
Ask for these labs

Fasting glucose and A1c are a start, but they can look "normal" for years. Fasting insulin and triglyceride-to-HDL ratio often reveal insulin resistance far earlier. Ask your doctor whether they're worth checking.

The good news: it's very responsive to food

Insulin resistance is largely driven by diet and lifestyle — which means the same everyday changes that flatten your blood sugar also help reverse it:

  • Eat in order and anchor with protein. Smaller spikes mean less insulin demand. Blood sugar 101 →
  • Build muscle. Muscle is your biggest glucose sponge — strength training directly improves insulin sensitivity.
  • Walk after meals. Ten minutes pulls glucose into your muscles with almost no insulin needed. Why it works →
  • Cut liquid sugar and ultra-processed food. The fastest spikes are the ones to remove first.
  • Prioritize sleep. A single bad night measurably worsens insulin sensitivity the next day.

None of this requires a crash diet. It's the steady, whole-food approach — done consistently — that turns the trend around. Catching insulin resistance early and acting on it is one of the highest-return things you can do for your long-term health.
