The playbook · Eating out & travel

How to eat out without undoing everything.

If your calendar is full of restaurants, airports, and client dinners, "just cook at home" isn't advice — it's a fantasy. Here's how to eat well on the road without white-knuckling it.

The one thing that quietly wrecks restaurant food

It's not the portion size and it's usually not the carbs. It's the oil. Nearly every restaurant — from fast food to fine dining — cooks in cheap, high-omega-6 seed oils because they're cheap and neutral. That's the single biggest reason a week of eating out can leave you puffy, stiff, and inflamed even when you ordered the "healthy" thing.

You can't control a restaurant kitchen. But you can steer toward the dishes where oil matters least, and that's most of the game.

The mindset shift

You're not trying to eat perfectly when you're out. You're trying to make the default choice a good one, most of the time. Aim for "better," not "flawless" — that's the whole difference between a plan you keep and one you quit.

Ordering: the simple hierarchy

  • Anchor on protein first. Steak, grilled chicken, fish, eggs. Decide your protein, then build the plate around it — this alone keeps you full and steadies your blood sugar.
  • Favor grilled, roasted, seared, or steamed over fried, breaded, or "crispy." Fried means a bath of seed oil.
  • Ask for butter or olive oil. Most kitchens will happily cook your fish or veg in butter if you ask. It's the easiest win on the menu.
  • Vegetables and a real starch — potatoes, rice, or bread you actually enjoy — are fine. Whole carbs aren't the enemy.
  • Dressing on the side. Bottled dressings are a seed-oil delivery system. Ask for oil and vinegar, or a wedge of lemon.

The blood-sugar tricks that still work out

Everything from the blood sugar guide travels with you:

  • Eat in order. Protein and vegetables first, the bread and dessert last. Same meal, a much smaller spike.
  • Skip the liquid sugar. Soda, juice, and sweet cocktails spike you fastest. Sparkling water, wine, or a spirit with soda are gentler picks.
  • Take a walk after. A ten-minute stroll after dinner — even a lap around the block — blunts the whole thing.

Travel: the parts nobody plans for

SituationThe move
AirportGrab jerky, a protein box, nuts, or Greek yogurt. Skip the pastry-and-latte combo that leaves you starving over the Atlantic.
On the planeEat before you fly so you're not at the mercy of the cart. Bring your own protein-forward snack.
Hotel roomStock the mini-fridge on arrival: fruit, yogurt, cheese, nuts. A $10 grocery run beats three days of vending machines.
Hotel breakfastEggs, meat, yogurt, fruit. Walk past the muffins and juice — they'll spike you before your first meeting.
Client dinnerOrder first so you set the tone, anchor on protein, enjoy yourself, and take the walk back to the hotel.

The 80/20 that keeps you sane

If you travel constantly, you will not eat perfectly, and trying to will just make you miserable and inconsistent. The goal is that your defaults are good — protein-first, seed-oil-aware, a walk after the big meal — so that the occasional indulgent dinner is a rounding error, not a spiral. Consistency over three weeks beats perfection over three days, every time.
