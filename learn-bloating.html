The root causes · Bloating

Bloating: why you feel like that at 3pm.

Bloated by midday even when you eat "healthy"? Your gut is sending a signal that something in your inputs is causing a problem. Here's how to read it — and what to do today.

What bloating actually is

Bloating is gas and fluid trapped in your digestive tract — usually because something disrupted how your gut is processing food. It's not just uncomfortable, and it's not random. Chronic bloating is your gut telling you that one of your inputs — a food, a habit, a stressor — is causing a problem it can't quietly handle.

The most common causes

  • Eating too fast. When you rush, you swallow air along with your food, and it has to go somewhere. Slow down and chew more — it's the simplest fix and it's free.
  • FODMAP overload. Fermentable carbohydrates — onions, garlic, beans, apples, wheat — feed your gut bacteria quickly and produce gas. High-FODMAP foods aren't bad for everyone, but if you're sensitive, these are often the culprits.
  • Too much raw fiber on a disrupted gut. Salads and raw vegetables are healthy, but a huge raw salad when your gut is already inflamed will make bloating worse, not better. Cooked vegetables are gentler while you heal.
  • Carbonated drinks. Even sparkling water adds gas to an already sensitive system. Worth cutting for a couple of weeks to see if it's a factor for you.
  • Seed oils and emulsifiers in processed food. These damage the gut lining (the tight junctions between cells), letting partially digested food particles into the bloodstream — which triggers immune responses and bloating.
  • Stress. Your gut has more neurons than your spinal cord. Cortisol directly slows digestion and disrupts gut motility. Eating at your desk under deadline pressure will bloat you regardless of what's on the plate.
  • Dysbiosis. An imbalanced microbiome — too many gas-producing bacteria, not enough beneficial ones — causes chronic bloating that no single food fix resolves. This one takes weeks of rebuilding, not a quick swap.
The reframe

Bloating isn't a flaw in your body — it's feedback. The goal isn't to suppress the signal, it's to find the input that's triggering it and change that.

Immediate fixes

  • Ginger — ginger tea or a little ginger before meals stimulates gastric motility and helps things move.
  • Enteric-coated peppermint oil capsules — shown to significantly reduce IBS-related bloating in clinical trials.
  • Digestive enzymes with meals — especially helpful when you're eating high-fiber foods your gut isn't used to.
  • Walk after eating — gentle movement helps clear gas and speeds digestion.
  • Stop eating ~2 hours before bed — your gut needs to rest too, and lying down on a full stomach traps gas.

Longer-term fixes

  • Cut seed oils and processed food for two weeks and observe what changes. This alone resolves a surprising amount of chronic bloating.
  • Try a low-FODMAP elimination for three weeks, then reintroduce foods systematically to find your specific triggers. Don't stay low-FODMAP forever — the goal is information, not permanent restriction.
  • Add fermented foods — kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, plain yogurt — to feed the beneficial bacteria and rebalance the microbiome over time.
  • Treat stress as a first-class input, not an afterthought. How you eat matters as much as what you eat — sit down, slow down, and give digestion a fighting chance.
A word of caution

Elimination diets are diagnostic tools, not lifestyles. The point is to narrow down your triggers and then reintroduce as much variety as your gut can handle. If bloating is severe, persistent, or comes with other symptoms, see a doctor — this is education, not medical advice.
