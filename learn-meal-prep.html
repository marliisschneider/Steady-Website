Meal prep without hating it.

The version of meal prep that burns everyone out is the rigid one: Sunday afternoon lost to cooking, five identical containers, and a quiet dread of Thursday's lunch. There's a better way, and it's more flexible, not less — you prep components, then assemble different meals all week.

Why rigid meal prep fails

Cooking one big batch of a single meal and eating it five days straight looks efficient and feels miserable by day three. You get bored, you waste the leftovers, and you swear off meal prep entirely — usually right back into takeout. The problem was never prep. It was locking your whole week into one dish you didn't even feel like eating.

Prep components, not meals

Here's the reframe that fixes it: don't cook meals ahead — cook building blocks. Spend the same hour batching a few versatile components, then spend two minutes each day assembling them into whatever you're in the mood for. Monday it's a rice bowl, Tuesday a wrap, Wednesday a big salad, Thursday a plate — same ingredients, four different meals, zero boredom.

The four things to batch

  • A protein (or two). Roast a couple of chicken breasts or thighs, brown some ground beef or turkey, hard-boil a half-dozen eggs, keep tins of salmon or sardines on hand.
  • A grain or starch. A pot of rice or quinoa, or a tray of roasted potatoes. Cook once; portion all week.
  • Vegetables, roasted and raw. Roast a big tray (they reheat well), and wash and chop some raw ones — cucumber, peppers, carrots, greens — for freshness and crunch.
  • One sauce or dressing. This is the secret weapon. A good sauce turns the same three components into a completely different meal. Make one jar; it carries the week.

The assembly mindset

Once your components are ready, weeknight "cooking" becomes weeknight assembling. Every good plate is the same simple formula: a protein, some vegetables, a smart carb, and something flavorful to tie it together. With your building blocks in the fridge, dinner is a two-minute mix — which is the whole point. You're not cooking when you're tired; you're plating.

Keep it flexible and realistic

Don't try to cover seven perfect days. Prep for three or four, leave room for a night out or leftovers, and simply cook once and eat twice when you make dinner — double the batch and tomorrow's lunch is done. Meal prep that bends around real life is the kind you'll actually keep.

  • Batch the four blocks. One protein, one grain, a tray of roasted veg, one sauce. That's the whole system.
  • Cook once, eat twice. Double any dinner you're already making and lunch prepares itself.
  • Store smart. Airtight containers, most components good for 3–4 days; freeze extras rather than forcing a full week.
  • Keep emergency components. Tinned fish, eggs, frozen vegetables, pre-cooked grains — a balanced meal in five minutes on the days prep didn't happen.
  • Don't over-prep. Three or four days beats a heroic seven that ends in waste and burnout.
Start here this weekend

Do the minimum version: roast one tray of chicken and one tray of vegetables, cook one pot of rice, and make one sauce. That's an hour, and you've got the makings of a week of two-minute meals — no identical containers in sight.
