Nutrition coach vs. dietitian: which do you need?

"Nutrition coach" and "registered dietitian" get used interchangeably, but they're different roles that solve different problems. Here's an honest breakdown — including when you genuinely need a dietitian instead of a coach.

The short version

A registered dietitian (RD/RDN) is a licensed medical professional who can diagnose and treat clinical conditions through nutrition. A nutrition coach focuses on behavior, habits, and day-to-day accountability — helping you actually do the things you already know you should. One is clinical; the other is practical. Many people are best served by knowing which problem they have.

What a registered dietitian does

  • Holds a degree, supervised training, and a license (a protected, regulated title).
  • Provides medical nutrition therapy for diagnosed conditions — kidney disease, eating disorders, complex diabetes, food allergies, feeding tubes, cancer care.
  • Often works in hospitals and clinics and may be covered by insurance with a referral.
  • Is who you want when nutrition is part of medical treatment.

What a nutrition coach does

  • Focuses on behavior change and consistency — the gap between knowing and doing.
  • Builds habits around your real life: your schedule, travel, tastes, and stress.
  • Offers accountability and support between sessions, not just a plan on paper.
  • Is who you want when you basically know what to do but can't make it stick — and you don't have a medical condition that needs clinical treatment.
You probably want a…If you…
Registered dietitianHave a diagnosed condition, an eating disorder, food allergies, or need insurance-covered medical nutrition therapy
Nutrition coachFeel tired, bloated, or stuck; know the basics but can't stay consistent; want habits built around your busy life
BothAre managing a condition and want ongoing day-to-day support — a coach who coordinates with your medical team
Where Steady fits

Steady is nutrition coaching. It's for capable people who feel worse than they should, know roughly what "healthy" means, and need someone to build it into their actual week and keep them consistent. It is not medical care — and if you have a diagnosed condition, coaching works best alongside your doctor or dietitian, never instead of them.

The honest test

Ask yourself one question: is my problem a lack of medical information, or a lack of consistency? If you need someone to treat a condition or interpret complex labs, see a registered dietitian or physician. If you already know that whole food, protein, and steady blood sugar are the answer — but life keeps getting in the way — that's exactly what a coach is for.

Titles and insurance rules for dietitians vary by country and state. This is general guidance, not medical or legal advice.
