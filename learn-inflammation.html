The root causes · Inflammation

Inflammation: the fire you can't see.

You can't see chronic inflammation, but you feel it — morning stiffness, brain fog, puffiness, that vaguely "off" feeling. Much of it comes from something hiding in nearly every packaged and restaurant food.

What inflammation actually is

Acute inflammation is good. It's how your body heals a cut or fights off an infection — redness, swelling, and then it resolves. That's the system working.

Chronic low-grade inflammation is different. It's your immune system stuck in the "on" position, quietly damaging tissue, disrupting hormones, and draining energy day after day. You can't see it, but you feel it: joint stiffness in the morning, skin flare-ups, brain fog, slow recovery from workouts, and a general sense of being "off" that you can't quite name.

What drives chronic inflammation

  • Seed oils (linoleic acid overload). Canola, soybean, sunflower, vegetable, corn, and safflower oils are extremely high in omega-6 polyunsaturated fat. When heated, they oxidize and produce toxic aldehydes (4-HNE, MDA) that trigger inflammatory cascades. These oils are in almost every restaurant dish, packaged food, and fast-food item. The average American now gets 80%+ of their fat from these oils — our great-grandparents got almost none.
  • Ultra-processed food. Anything with more than five ingredients you can't picture in their natural state. Emulsifiers, artificial dyes, and preservatives disrupt the gut lining and trigger an immune response.
  • Sugar and refined carbs. Excess fructose — especially from liquid sources like juice and soda — is processed in the liver and generates inflammatory byproducts. High-fructose corn syrup is particularly problematic.
  • Fast food. A fast-food meal has been shown to trigger an immune response within four hours that's measurably similar to a bacterial infection. Researchers at the University of Bonn (2018) found it made the innate immune system more aggressive — and that response persisted for weeks afterward.

What seed oils are actually doing in your body

When omega-6 fats dominate your cell membranes — as they do on a modern diet — your body produces more pro-inflammatory signaling molecules called prostaglandins. The ideal ratio of omega-6 to omega-3 is roughly 4:1. The modern American diet runs closer to 20:1. That imbalance is directly associated with cardiovascular disease, depression, autoimmune conditions, and accelerated aging.

What to look for in the store

Check the ingredients of every packaged food. If it lists canola, soybean, vegetable, sunflower, safflower, or corn oil — put it back. This includes chips, crackers, salad dressings, mayo, granola bars, bread, plant-based meat, and most "healthy" packaged snacks. Cook with olive oil, butter, ghee, avocado oil, or coconut oil instead.

The 10-second habit

Turn the package over before it goes in the cart. You'll be shocked how often seed oil is the second or third ingredient in food marketed as healthy. Reading labels is the whole game.

Foods that actively reduce inflammation

  • Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) — high in EPA and DHA omega-3s, which directly counteract inflammatory pathways.
  • Extra virgin olive oil — contains oleocanthal, which inhibits COX enzymes the same way ibuprofen does.
  • Blueberries — anthocyanins reduce NF-κB, a master inflammatory switch.
  • Turmeric + black pepper — curcumin suppresses multiple inflammatory markers; black pepper boosts its absorption by roughly 2000%.
  • Leafy greens (spinach, kale, arugula) — magnesium deficiency is strongly linked to inflammation, and greens are the best source.
  • Walnuts — the best plant source of ALA omega-3; also shown to reduce CRP, a key inflammatory marker.
  • Green tea — EGCG is one of the most-studied anti-inflammatory compounds in the diet.
