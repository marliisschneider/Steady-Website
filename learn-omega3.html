Omega-3s: the fat that fights the fire.

Two families of fat quietly pull your body in opposite directions — one turns inflammation up, the other turns it down. Modern eating has flooded us with the first and starved us of the second. Rebalancing them is one of the simplest anti-inflammatory moves you can make.

It's not omega-3 vs. nothing — it's a balance

Your body uses two families of polyunsaturated fat: omega-6 and omega-3. You need both. The problem is the ratio. Omega-6 fats — abundant in the seed oils hiding in nearly every packaged and restaurant food — tend to push toward inflammation. Omega-3 fats push the other way, toward calming and resolving it. For most of human history those two came in roughly even amounts. Today many people eat something like ten to twenty times more omega-6 than omega-3. That lopsided ratio keeps the inflammatory dial turned up in the background.

What omega-3s actually do

Beyond dialing down inflammation, the marine omega-3s (EPA and DHA) are structural building blocks — DHA is a major component of your brain and eyes, and both are used to make the signals that resolve inflammation once it's done its job. That's why healthy omega-3 levels show up across so many systems: steadier mood, sharper focus, calmer joints, and better heart and metabolic markers. It's not a single-symptom nutrient; it's infrastructure.

Not all omega-3s are equal

This is the part people get wrong. There are two practical kinds:

  • EPA and DHA — the active marine forms, found in fatty fish. These are what your body actually uses.
  • ALA — the plant form, in flax, chia, and walnuts. Good to eat, but your body converts only a small fraction of it into EPA and DHA.

So plant sources help, but they're not a full substitute for the marine forms. If you eat no fish at all, that's the gap to plan around.

Where to actually get them

The best sources are small, oily, cold-water fish — easy to remember as SMASH: salmon, mackerel, anchovies, sardines, herring. Two to three servings a week covers most people. Canned sardines and canned salmon are some of the cheapest, most convenient nutrition you can buy — no cooking, long shelf life, and loaded with the marine forms.

What about fish oil?

A quality fish oil (or algae oil, if you're plant-based) is a reasonable backup if you genuinely don't eat fish — it's one of the better-supported supplements. But it's a backup, not a replacement for real food, and quality varies a lot. Food first; supplement to fill a real gap.

How to rebalance the ratio

  • Eat fatty fish 2–3 times a week. The single highest-impact move. Fresh, frozen, or canned all count.
  • Keep canned sardines or salmon on hand. On toast, in a salad, straight from the tin — the easiest cheap win in the whole library.
  • Cut the seed oils. Rebalancing works from both ends: bringing omega-6 down matters as much as bringing omega-3 up. This is where the cooking-oils guide and label reading pay off.
  • Add flax, chia, and walnuts. They won't replace fish, but they help — a tablespoon of ground flax or chia in oats or yogurt is an easy daily add.
  • If you don't eat fish, consider a quality omega-3 supplement. Fish oil or, for plant-based eaters, algae-based EPA/DHA.
Start here this week

Buy two tins of sardines or wild salmon and eat them for two lunches this week. That alone moves your ratio more than any supplement — for a couple of dollars.

One caution: high-dose fish oil can thin the blood, so if you're on blood thinners or heading into surgery, check with your doctor before supplementing. Getting omega-3s from food is safe for essentially everyone.
