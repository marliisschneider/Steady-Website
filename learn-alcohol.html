Alcohol, honestly: what that drink actually does.

This isn't a lecture, and it isn't "never drink again." It's the honest mechanism — what happens to your sleep, your blood sugar, and your next day — so you can make the trade on purpose instead of wondering why a couple of glasses leaves you flat for two days.

To your body, alcohol is a priority problem

Your body treats alcohol as a mild toxin it needs to clear, and it moves it to the front of the line. Until the alcohol is dealt with, a lot of other work pauses — including burning fat for fuel and steadily managing your blood sugar. That's why a few drinks don't just add calories; they change how your body handles everything you ate with them. The drink itself isn't the whole story. The knock-on effects are.

The sleep tax is the big one

This is the effect most people feel and least connect to the drink. Alcohol is a sedative, so it helps you fall asleep faster — which is exactly why the nightcap fools people. But as it wears off in the second half of the night, it fragments your sleep and suppresses REM, the dreaming stage your brain uses to consolidate memory and regulate mood. You can be technically in bed eight hours and wake up feeling like you slept five.

If you've read the 3am wake-up guide, this is often the culprit: the drink helps you drop off, then the rebound wakes you at 3 or 4 and won't let you back down.

The blood sugar rollercoaster

Sugary drinks — cocktails, mixers, dessert wines — spike your blood sugar like any fast carb, setting up the crash that follows. But alcohol has a second trick: while your liver is busy clearing it, it does a worse job of releasing stored glucose to keep your blood sugar up overnight. So on top of the spike from the mixer, you can dip too low in the early morning — which, as with any crash, triggers the stress hormones that jolt you awake and leave you craving carbs the next day. That "I was so good yesterday, why am I starving and reaching for pastries" morning after drinks is chemistry, not weakness.

Why you're puffy and foggy the next day

Alcohol is a diuretic — it pulls water out of you — which is behind a lot of the next-morning puffiness, headache, and dry-mouth fog. It's also mildly inflammatory and can irritate the gut lining, so if you already deal with bloating or an "off" feeling, drinks tend to amplify it. None of this is dramatic on its own; it's the stack that adds up to feeling flat for a day or two after what felt like a modest night.

It's not all equal

Before the fixes, the honest nuance: what, how, and when you drink changes the cost enormously. A sugary cocktail on an empty stomach right before bed is close to the worst case. The same amount of alcohol as dry wine, with food, earlier in the evening, with water alongside, costs you a fraction as much. You have far more control than "drink or don't."

How to drink smarter

  • Eat first — protein and fat. Never drink on an empty stomach. Food slows alcohol absorption and blunts the blood-sugar swing. A real meal before the first drink changes the whole night.
  • Choose lower-sugar options. Dry wine, spirits with soda water and lime, or a light beer beat sugary cocktails and mixers by a mile. The mixer is often doing more damage than the alcohol.
  • Water between drinks. One glass of water per drink handles most of the dehydration that becomes tomorrow's headache and puffiness — and naturally paces you.
  • Finish earlier. The closer to bedtime, the bigger the sleep tax. Giving your body two or three hours between the last drink and sleep meaningfully protects the second half of your night.
  • Cap it, and mean it. Decide the number before you start, not at drink two. The drop-off in how you feel the next day is steep after the first couple.
  • Bank alcohol-free nights. The single biggest lever isn't perfecting any one night — it's how many nights a week you don't drink at all. Your sleep, energy, and gut all recover on the off nights.
Start here tonight

If you're drinking, do just two things: eat a real meal first, and match every drink with a glass of water. Notice how different tomorrow morning feels — that alone tells you how much of "just tired" was really the drink.

One honest note: if alcohol has become the thing that gets you to sleep or takes the edge off most nights, or if cutting back feels genuinely hard, that's worth talking to your doctor about — it's a common pattern and not a willpower failing. And in pregnancy, the guidance is simple and not up for nuance: none.
