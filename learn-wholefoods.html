Back to basics: the whole-food reset.

I chased every trend — vegan, keto, high-protein, no dairy — and stayed inflamed and bloated for years. The thing that finally worked was almost embarrassingly simple. Here's the framework, and why it beats the fancy stuff.

The trap of "optimized" eating

I know the feeling of doing everything right and getting nothing back. I rowed at the highest levels for a decade, and I treated food like an athlete — carefully, seriously, constantly reading. Vegan for a while. Then keto. Then very high protein. I cut dairy completely because I'd heard it caused inflammation. And through all of it, my joints hurt, I was bloated, and I felt vaguely off no matter what.

The lesson wasn't that I lacked discipline. It's that I was optimizing the wrong layer. Every trend added rules and subtracted foods, but none of them asked the simpler question: is this actually food my body recognizes? When I finally stripped it back to whole, real food — and put dairy back in — my joint pain and bloating were gone within a month. After years.

The one-line version

Eat food your great-grandmother would recognize. Cut the two things she never ate: industrial seed oils and ultra-processed products. Almost everything else takes care of itself.

What "whole food" actually means

A whole food is one ingredient, or a short list of ingredients you can picture in their natural state. An apple is a whole food. Butter is essentially one. A pasture-raised egg, a piece of salmon, a bag of spinach, a wheel of real cheese — whole foods. The test isn't "healthy" or "clean," which are marketing words. The test is: would this have existed as food 150 years ago?

What to keep

  • Real animal foods. Meat, fish, eggs, and full-fat dairy — milk, grass-fed butter, real cheese, plain yogurt. Naturally rich in protein and the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, K2) that are hard to get elsewhere.
  • Vegetables and fruit. As close to whole as possible. Fiber, minerals, and polyphenols, in the form your gut evolved to handle.
  • Stable, natural fats. Butter, ghee, extra-virgin olive oil, coconut oil, tallow. These don't oxidize the way industrial oils do.
  • Whole starches, to your tolerance. Potatoes, rice, oats, fruit, sourdough. Real carbohydrate isn't the enemy — refined and liquid sugar is.

What to cut

Why dairy went back in for me

Cutting dairy is one of the most common "clean eating" moves, and for a small number of people with a true intolerance it's the right call. But for most people, blanket-cutting a whole, traditional food removes a dense source of protein, calcium, and fat-soluble vitamins — and adds stress for no benefit. When I added full-fat dairy back alongside cutting seed oils, nothing got worse. Everything got better. The problem was almost never the foods humans have eaten for millennia. It was the industrial stuff we added in the last seventy years.

A note on individuality

Whole food is the foundation for everyone. The details — how much starch, whether dairy sits well with you, how much fruit — are individual. That's exactly the part coaching is for: finding your version instead of copying someone else's.

How to make it survive a real week

  • Win at the store, not at the table. If it's not in the house, the 9pm decision makes itself. Shop the perimeter; read the label on anything from the middle aisles.
  • Anchor every meal with protein. Eggs, meat, fish, yogurt, cheese. Protein is what keeps you full and stops the mid-afternoon hunt for sugar.
  • Cook at home more often than not. Not because home food is magic, but because it's the only place you control the oil. That single lever removes most seed oil from your diet automatically.
  • Keep three easy defaults. A breakfast, a lunch, and a dinner you can make on autopilot. Consistency comes from removing decisions, not adding willpower.

This isn't a 30-day cleanse you white-knuckle and then quit. It's a default you can keep for the rest of your life, because it's just real food eaten in a way that fits your week. Start here. Everything else in the library builds on it.
