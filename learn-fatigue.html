The basics · Energy

Why am I so tired all the time?

You sleep seven or eight hours and still drag through the day. You're not lazy and you're probably not just "getting older." Most everyday fatigue traces back to a handful of fixable things — and none of them is willpower.

If you've had persistent, unexplained exhaustion, it's worth ruling out medical causes with your doctor — thyroid issues, anemia, and sleep apnea are real and treatable. But for the everyday "why am I always tired" that so many high-functioning people live with, the cause is usually on your plate. Here are the four biggest ones.

1. Your blood sugar is on a rollercoaster

A breakfast of cereal, toast, or a pastry and coffee spikes your blood sugar, triggers a flood of insulin, and drops you into a crash an hour or two later. Every crash feels like fatigue — so you reach for more caffeine or sugar, spike again, and ride that wave all day. Flatten the wave and steady energy is what's left. How to flatten it →

The fastest test

For three days, start your day with 30g of protein instead of carbs — eggs, Greek yogurt, or leftovers. Most people are startled by how much steadier the whole day feels. That's your blood sugar telling you something.

2. You're not eating enough protein

Protein is what keeps you full and level. Most "healthy" breakfasts and grab-and-go lunches are almost pure carbohydrate with barely any. Under-eating protein leaves you hungry, snacky, and foggy — and slowly costs you the muscle that keeps your metabolism humming. How much you actually need →

3. Low-grade inflammation is draining you

Chronic, low-grade inflammation is a quiet energy tax — and one of its biggest drivers is the seed oils in nearly every packaged and restaurant food. That "off," heavy, foggy feeling that no amount of sleep fixes is often inflammation, not tiredness. Cut the seed oils and many people feel a lightness return within weeks. The seed-oil problem →

4. You sit still after you eat

Eating a big meal and then sitting for hours is a recipe for the afternoon slump. A 10-minute walk after your biggest meal pulls glucose out of your blood and into your muscles, softening the crash before it starts. It's the single easiest energy upgrade there is. Why the walk works →

Your one-week experiment

1. 30g of protein at breakfast. 2. Cook in butter or olive oil, not seed oils. 3. A 10-minute walk after lunch or dinner. 4. Water before more coffee. Give it a week and see how "tired" you actually are.

The thing about "tired"

Constant fatigue isn't your baseline and it isn't your fault. It's feedback. When smart, capable people fix these four things, the exhaustion they'd learned to live with usually just lifts — and they realize how long they'd been running on empty without knowing it.

This is general education, not medical advice. Persistent or severe fatigue should be checked out by a doctor to rule out underlying conditions.
