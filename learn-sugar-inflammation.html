Sugar and the slow fire.

Everyone knows sugar isn't great for you. Fewer people know why — that beyond the spike-and-crash, a steady drip of added sugar quietly keeps your body in a low-grade inflamed state. It's not about fearing an occasional treat. It's about the background dose you don't notice.

How sugar lights the fire

Three things happen when added sugar runs high, day after day:

  • Glycation. Excess glucose literally sticks to proteins in your body, forming damaged compounds (AGEs) that your immune system treats as irritants. It's part of why chronically high blood sugar ages skin and stiffens blood vessels.
  • Oxidative stress. Big glucose surges generate a burst of cellular "exhaust" that triggers inflammatory signals.
  • The spike-crash loop. Each sharp blood sugar spike and crash is itself a small stressor — and it drives you back toward more sugar, keeping the cycle going.

None of this is dramatic in a single sitting. It's the repetition — several times a day, most days — that keeps the dial turned up.

Fruit is not the problem

Let's be clear about the target. This is about added and refined sugar — the sugar poured into drinks, sauces, snacks, and baked goods — not the sugar that comes packaged inside whole fruit with its fiber, water, and nutrients. An apple and a can of soda are not the same thing to your body, even if a label counts similar grams. Whole fruit stays on the "yes" list.

Where the sugar actually hides

Most people's sugar isn't coming from obvious dessert. It's riding along in food marketed as healthy — which is exactly why reading the label is the whole game.

The gut angle

There's one more mechanism worth knowing: a diet high in refined sugar tends to feed the less-helpful bacteria in your gut and crowd out the beneficial ones, which can weaken the gut lining and add to the inflammatory load. It's another reason the fix is "more real food," not "one magic cut."

How to cut it painlessly

  • Kill liquid sugar first. Soda, juice, sweet coffee drinks, and bottled smoothies are the highest-impact, easiest cuts — you barely notice them as food, and they hit hardest. Start here.
  • Turn the package over. Scan the ingredients for sugar under its many names (cane juice, syrups, anything ending in "-ose"). It's in sauces, dressings, bread, and "healthy" snacks far more than you'd guess.
  • Never eat sugar naked. If you're having something sweet, pair it with protein, fat, or fiber to blunt the spike. Fruit with nuts beats fruit alone; dessert after a real meal beats dessert on an empty stomach.
  • Keep whole fruit on the menu. When a craving hits, whole fruit satisfies the sweet tooth with fiber and water attached. It's the swap, not the enemy.
  • Give your palate two weeks. Taste recalibrates. Foods that seem barely sweet now will taste plenty sweet once you've dialed down the background level — and the old stuff starts to taste cloying.
Start here today

Cut one liquid sugar source — the soda, the sweet latte, the juice — and replace it with water, sparkling water, or unsweetened coffee. It's the single biggest sugar reduction most people can make in one move.

This isn't about fear or perfection. An occasional real dessert, enjoyed and moved on from, isn't stoking any fire. It's the daily, invisible dose — the drinks and the hidden grams — that quietly matters. Handle those, and the treats take care of themselves.
