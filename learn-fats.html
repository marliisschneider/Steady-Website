Fats, explained.

Fat spent forty years as the villain of the nutrition story — and it turned out we were mostly blaming the wrong thing. The honest picture is simpler than the fear ever was: it's about the type of fat, and once you can tell them apart, most of the anxiety disappears.

The old fear was aimed at the wrong target

When fat got blamed for heart disease, the food industry gave us "low-fat" everything — and to make low-fat food edible, they packed it with sugar and refined starch. We swapped fat for sugar, and as a population got sicker, not healthier. Meanwhile fat is not optional: your body needs it to build hormones, run your brain, absorb key vitamins, and feel satisfied after a meal. The goal was never "less fat." It was "the right fats."

The three families of fat

You don't need to count anything. You just need to know who's who.

  • Monounsaturated — eat freely. Olive oil, avocado, most nuts. This is the least controversial, most clearly beneficial fat there is. Make extra-virgin olive oil a default.
  • Polyunsaturated — it depends which. Two sub-types pull in opposite directions: omega-3s (from fatty fish — anti-inflammatory, most people need more) and omega-6 (fine in whole foods, but inflammatory in the excess we get from seed oils). Getting this balance right is one of the highest-value moves in the whole library.
  • Saturated — the nuanced one. Butter, full-fat dairy, meat, coconut. This is the fat that took the hardest hit, and the science has softened a lot: in the context of a whole-food diet, it's not the villain it was made out to be. That's not a license for unlimited — quality and context matter — but real butter on real food is not the problem it was sold as.

And one genuine villain: trans fats — the artificial "partially hydrogenated" oils in some old-school processed and fried foods. These are the fats that really do drive heart disease. They're largely banned now, but scan labels for "partially hydrogenated" and avoid it.

The cholesterol update

For most people, the cholesterol you eat (in eggs, for instance) has surprisingly little effect on the cholesterol in your blood — far less than the old "avoid egg yolks" advice assumed. The bigger dietary drivers of poor cholesterol numbers tend to be refined carbs, sugar, and trans fats, not whole-food fats. That said, a minority of people are genuine "hyper-responders," and some have inherited lipid conditions — so this is a place to know your own numbers rather than assume.

The real fat problem is industrial

If there's a fat to actually worry about, it's not the butter or the avocado — it's the industrial seed oils in nearly every packaged and restaurant food, and the fats damaged by deep-frying and heavy processing. That's the throughline of this whole site: the natural fats your great-grandmother cooked with are fine; it's the factory versions that cause trouble.

How to eat fat well

  • Cook with stable, natural fats. Butter, extra-virgin olive oil, avocado oil. See the cooking oils guide for which fat suits which heat.
  • Eat whole-food fats. Avocado, nuts, seeds, fatty fish, full-fat dairy, olives. They come with nutrients and keep you full.
  • Ditch the seed oils and fried stuff. This single swap does more than obsessing over the fat in whole foods ever will.
  • Don't fear the fat in real food. Full-fat yogurt beats sweetened low-fat. Fat is what makes real food satisfying — and satisfaction is what keeps you from grazing on junk an hour later.
  • Mind the omega balance. More omega-3 (fish), less omega-6 (seed oils). It's the ratio that matters most.
Start here today

Make one swap: use butter or olive oil where you'd have used a seed oil (canola, soybean, "vegetable" oil), and buy the full-fat version of one thing you usually buy low-fat. Fat isn't the enemy — the industrial version is.

One caveat: if you have an inherited cholesterol condition (like familial hypercholesterolemia) or specific lipid concerns, saturated-fat guidance really is individual — work from your own bloodwork with your doctor rather than any general rule, including this one.
