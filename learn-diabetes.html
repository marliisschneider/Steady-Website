The basics · Blood sugar

Blood sugar 101 — the thing no one taught you.

When my grandmother was diagnosed with diabetes, I realized how few people — smart, health-conscious people — actually understand how blood sugar works. It's simpler than it sounds, and understanding it changes everything.

Important

This is education, not medical advice. If you have diabetes or take any blood-sugar medication, work with your doctor before changing how you eat — food changes can affect how much medication you need, so those adjustments must be supervised.

What blood sugar actually is

Every time you eat carbohydrates — bread, rice, fruit, sugar — your body breaks them down into glucose, which enters your bloodstream. In response, your pancreas releases insulin, a hormone that ushers that glucose into your cells to be used for energy. When this system runs smoothly, your blood sugar rises gently and settles back down. You barely notice.

The trouble starts when it rises too fast and too high — a spike — which forces a big surge of insulin, which then drops your blood sugar too low: the crash. That rollercoaster is what you feel as the 3pm slump, the sudden sugar craving, the brain fog, the "hangry" mood.

Why it matters more than you think

Occasional spikes are normal. But when they happen meal after meal, year after year, your cells slowly stop responding to insulin as well — a state called insulin resistance. That's the road toward prediabetes and type 2 diabetes. The encouraging part: the same daily habits that flatten your spikes are the ones that protect that system over time.

The three levers you actually control

You don't have to give up carbs or count anything. You just change how you eat them.

  • Eat in order. Start your meal with protein and vegetables, and save the starch or sweet for last. The exact same food, eaten in this order, produces a noticeably smaller spike — because the fiber, protein, and fat slow how fast glucose hits your blood.
  • Move after you eat. A 10-minute walk in the hour after a meal lets your muscles pull glucose out of your bloodstream without much insulin at all — often lowering the spike by 15–30%. You don't need a gym; you need to not sit down.
  • Anchor every meal with protein and fat. A breakfast of just oatmeal and fruit is almost pure glucose. Add eggs, yogurt, or nuts and the whole curve flattens — and you stay full for hours instead of crashing by mid-morning.

What quietly makes it worse

  • Liquid sugar. Soda, juice, and sweetened coffee drinks are the fastest, sharpest spikes there are — sugar with no fiber to slow it down.
  • Naked carbs. Refined carbs eaten alone — a plain bagel, a bowl of cereal, crackers by themselves — hit hardest. Pair them with protein and fat and they behave completely differently.
  • Skipping meals, then overeating. Long gaps followed by a big fast meal create the biggest swings. Steady beats heroic.
The whole idea in one line

You can't always change what is on the table, but you can almost always change the order you eat it, whether you walk after, and what you pair it with. That's most of the game.

If diabetes runs in your family

A family history isn't a sentence — it's a heads-up. The habits above are exactly what help keep the system healthy, and my grandmother is proof that it's never too late to feel better: within a few months of learning food order, post-meal walks, and real whole food, she had her energy and her quality of life back. If you already have a diagnosis, use this to have a better conversation with your doctor — not to replace them.
