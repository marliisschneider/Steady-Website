The playbook · Groceries

How to read a food label in 10 seconds.

You don't need a nutrition degree to shop well — you need one habit. Turn the package over and run a quick three-part scan. It's the highest-leverage grocery skill there is.

Rule zero: ignore the front

The front of the package is marketing, not information. "All natural," "heart healthy," "made with real fruit," "high protein," "keto-friendly" — these are largely unregulated feel-good phrases designed to sell. The truth is on the back, in the ingredients list. Flip it over every time; that one move is 80% of the game.

The 10-second scan

1. Scan for seed oils.   2. Count the ingredients you can't picture.   3. Check the added sugar. If it fails any of the three, put it back.

Step 1 — Scan for seed oils

Run your eye down the ingredients for any of these. If you spot one, choose something else:

  • Canola (rapeseed) oil
  • Soybean oil — the most common; it's in almost everything
  • "Vegetable oil" — vague, usually soybean or a blend
  • Sunflower, safflower, corn, cottonseed, grapeseed oil
  • "Partially hydrogenated" anything — trans fats

These industrial oils are a major, under-discussed driver of inflammation, and they hide in food marketed as healthy — hummus, "veggie" chips, salad dressing, granola bars, plant-based meat, and most packaged snacks. More on why seed oils matter →

Step 2 — Count the mystery ingredients

Read the list and ask: can I picture each of these in its natural form? Butter, oats, salt, tomatoes — yes. Sodium stearoyl lactylate, maltodextrin, "natural flavors," and a string of dyes and emulsifiers — no. A short list of recognizable ingredients is a good sign. A long list of chemical names is a processed-food red flag.

Green flagRed flag
5 or fewer recognizable ingredientsA paragraph of ingredients
Butter, olive oil, sea saltCanola / soybean / "vegetable" oil

Step 3 — Check the added sugar

Look at the "added sugars" line and the ingredients. Sugar hides under dozens of names — corn syrup, cane juice, dextrose, maltose, agave, fruit-juice concentrate. Ingredients are listed by weight, so if any sugar appears in the first three, the product is essentially a dessert. This matters most for the things pretending not to be sweet: bread, pasta sauce, yogurt, granola, "healthy" bars.

The whole habit in one line

Turn it over. Seed oil? Put it back. Ingredients you can't picture? Put it back. Sugar in the top three? Put it back. Do that for a month and your cart transforms without a single rule about calories.

The best label is no label

The foods that need no scanning at all are the ones without a label: eggs, meat, fish, vegetables, fruit, butter, plain dairy. Fill your cart from the perimeter of the store first, and you'll only need this scan for the few packaged things you actually buy.
