The root causes · Gut

How to improve your gut health.

Your gut affects your digestion, your immune system, your inflammation, and even your mood. The good news: improving your gut health doesn't take an expensive shelf of supplements — it takes a handful of everyday food habits.

Why your gut matters more than you think

Your gut is home to trillions of bacteria that help digest food, make certain vitamins, train your immune system, and keep the gut lining healthy. When that community is diverse and well-fed, things run smoothly. When it's starved of fiber and battered by ultra-processed food, you get the familiar signs: bloating, irregularity, that heavy "off" feeling, and more system-wide inflammation. More on bloating →

What actually improves it

  • Eat more fiber, from more plants. Your gut bacteria feed on fiber. Variety matters as much as amount — aim for lots of different vegetables, fruit, legumes, nuts, and seeds across the week. Diversity on your plate builds diversity in your gut.
  • Add fermented foods. Plain yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, and other traditional ferments deliver live cultures that support your microbiome. A few spoonfuls most days does more than most probiotic pills.
  • Cut seed oils and ultra-processed food. Industrial oils, emulsifiers, and additives can irritate the gut lining and disrupt the bacteria living there. Removing them is often what fixes "mystery" gut issues. Why seed oils →
  • Ease off the sugar. A diet high in sugar and refined carbs feeds the wrong microbes. Steadier blood sugar tends to mean a happier gut.
  • Slow down and chew. Digestion starts in your mouth. Eating fast, on the go, and stressed leaves more work for your gut — and causes a lot of bloating blamed on food.
  • Manage stress and sleep. The gut and brain are in constant conversation. Chronic stress and poor sleep genuinely disrupt digestion.
Food before supplements

A daily bowl of plain yogurt with berries and a spoon of seeds does more for most people's guts than a cupboard of probiotic capsules. Feed the good bacteria with real food and they take care of the rest.

The "30 plants a week" idea

A simple, research-inspired target that beats counting anything: aim for 30 different plant foods a week. Vegetables, fruit, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds, herbs, and spices all count. It sounds like a lot until you realize a single salad or stir-fry can knock out six or seven. Chasing variety naturally crowds out the processed stuff and feeds a wider range of good bacteria.

When to see a doctor

Persistent pain, blood in your stool, unexplained weight loss, or symptoms that don't improve with better food deserve a proper medical work-up. Food fixes a lot — but not everything.

Improving your gut health isn't a cleanse or a 30-day protocol. It's diverse plants, some fermented food, and cutting the industrial stuff — kept up over time. Do that and the bloating, irregularity, and heaviness usually settle on their own.

General education, not medical advice. Persistent digestive symptoms should be evaluated by a doctor.
