Carbs: friend or foe?

It's the wrong question. Carbs got demonized into a single scary category, but a bowl of lentils and a handful of gummy bears are both "carbs" — and they do almost opposite things to your body. The useful question isn't whether to eat carbs. It's which ones, how much, and with what.

"Carb" is not one food

This is the whole misunderstanding in one line. "Carbohydrate" spans everything from broccoli and black beans to white bread and table sugar. Lumping them together and asking "are carbs bad?" is like asking "are liquids bad?" — water and gasoline are both liquids. The category is too broad to have one answer.

The real divide: whole vs. refined

Instead of a good/bad split, picture a spectrum:

Whole carbs — vegetables, fruit, legumes, whole grains, potatoes with their skin — come packaged with fiber, water, and nutrients. That fiber slows everything down, so they release their sugar gently. Refined carbs — white bread, pastries, most packaged snacks, and sugar itself — have been stripped of all that, so they hit your bloodstream fast and hard. It's the same underlying nutrient; the packaging changes everything.

It's about the spike, not the carb

Here's the reframe that makes carbs simple: what matters most is how fast the sugar arrives, and that's largely in your control. The exact same carb causes a much smaller blood sugar spike when you eat it with protein, fat, and fiber — and when you save it for the end of the meal. A slice of bread with eggs is a different event than a slice of bread alone. So the goal isn't to fear carbs; it's to stop eating them naked.

Do you actually need "low-carb"?

Honestly, most people don't. Whole carbs are not something to be afraid of — and if you're active, you genuinely need them for energy and recovery. Where lower-carb eating earns its place is for specific situations: people with insulin resistance, prediabetes, or diabetes often do better with fewer, gentler carbs (see insulin resistance and prediabetes). It's a tool for some, not a moral rule for everyone. Carbs aren't a character flaw.

How to eat carbs well

  • Choose whole over refined, most of the time. The potato with its skin over the fries, the fruit over the juice, the whole grain over the white. Same food family, far better version.
  • Never eat carbs naked. Pair them with protein, fat, or fiber every time. It flattens the spike and keeps you full.
  • Save the starch for last. Vegetables and protein first, the bread or rice at the end. Same plate, smaller curve.
  • Match carbs to movement. More around active days and workouts, fewer on sedentary ones. Your body handles them best when it's going to use them.
  • Cut the liquid and refined stuff first. If you're going to reduce anything, start with soda, juice, and sugary snacks — the fastest spikes with the least nourishment.
Start here today

Don't cut carbs — reorder them. At your next meal, eat the protein and vegetables first and the carb last. Same food, gentler curve, steadier afternoon.

One note: if you have diabetes or prediabetes, the amount of carbohydrate matters more for you than for most, and it's worth dialing in with your doctor or a dietitian rather than eyeballing it.
