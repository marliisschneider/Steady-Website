The root causes · Blood Sugar

Blood sugar: what's actually happening.

The 3pm crash, the brain fog, the sudden craving for something sweet — it's not a willpower problem. It's a glucose problem. And it's one of the most fixable things in your day.

What blood sugar actually is

Your cells run on glucose. When you eat, blood sugar rises, and your pancreas releases insulin to move that glucose out of your blood and into your cells. That's the system working correctly.

The problem: most people eat in a way that causes sharp spikes followed by sharp crashes. That crash is the 3pm slump. It's the brain fog after lunch, the irritability, the craving for something sweet at exactly the wrong time. You're not weak. Your blood sugar just fell off a cliff, and your body is screaming for fast fuel to climb back up.

Why it matters beyond energy

The daily slump is the symptom you notice. The damage you don't notice is bigger. Chronic blood sugar spikes drive inflammation, accelerate aging (glucose binds to collagen and arteries in a process called glycation — it's why high blood sugar ages skin and stiffens blood vessels), disrupt sleep, and are a direct precursor to type 2 diabetes.

Here's the part most people miss: you don't have to be diabetic for this to be hurting you. Most people with dysregulated blood sugar don't feel "diabetic." They feel tired, foggy, and puffy — and they assume that's just normal now.

What causes the spikes

  • Eating carbohydrates alone — especially processed ones: bread, pasta, rice, pastries, juice
  • Skipping meals, then eating one large one
  • Eating in the wrong order — carbs first, protein last
  • Sitting still for long stretches right after eating

How to flatten the curve

None of this requires eating less or cutting out foods you love. It's mostly about sequencing and timing — small changes with an outsized effect.

  • Eat in order. Start every meal with protein and fat, then fiber, then carbs. This dramatically slows glucose absorption. A salad before pasta. Eggs before toast. Same meal, far smaller spike.
  • Move after eating. A 10-minute walk or 20 squats after a meal can blunt the glucose spike by up to 30% (Diabetes Care, 2022). You don't need a gym — you need to not sit down for ten minutes.
  • Don't eat carbs naked. Pair every carb with protein, fat, or fiber. Fruit with nut butter. Bread with eggs. Rice with meat and vegetables. The carb still gets eaten — it just enters your bloodstream gently.
  • Vinegar before a carb-heavy meal. One tablespoon of apple cider vinegar in water before eating slows gastric emptying and reduces the spike. Supported by multiple randomized controlled trials.
  • Protect your sleep. A single night of poor sleep increases insulin resistance the next day. The effect is measurable and immediate — you'll spike higher on the exact same breakfast.
Start here today

Pick one meal. Eat the protein and vegetables first, save the carbs for last, and take a 10-minute walk after. That's it. Notice whether the afternoon feels different.
