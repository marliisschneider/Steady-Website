The root causes · Gut

Is it really gluten? Or is it everything around it?

A lot of people are convinced they're gluten intolerant — bloated and miserable after bread. Sometimes it's genuinely gluten. But very often the real culprit is the processed food, seed oils, and additives that modern bread comes wrapped in.

First, rule out the serious thing

Celiac disease is real, serious, and different from this. If you suspect it, get properly tested by a doctor before you cut out gluten — testing only works while gluten is still in your diet. This article is about the far more common "I feel bad after bread but I'm not celiac" situation.

The gluten assumption

Here's the usual story: you feel bloated and off after sandwiches, pasta, or pastries. You cut gluten, you feel better, and you conclude gluten is the enemy. But look at what you actually removed along with the gluten — because it was rarely just the wheat.

What was riding along with the bread

Modern packaged bread and baked goods are some of the most processed food in the store. When you cut them, you also cut:

  • Seed oils. Soybean and canola oil are in most commercial bread, crackers, and baked goods — a major, under-discussed driver of inflammation and gut irritation.
  • Emulsifiers and dough conditioners. Additives that extend shelf life can disrupt the gut lining and the bacteria living in it.
  • Fast-rise, low-ferment production. Cheap bread is made in hours, not days — which leaves in compounds (like certain fructans) that a slow ferment would have broken down.
  • The whole meal it came with. The bread was a sandwich with processed deli meat, or pasta drowned in a jarred, seed-oil-heavy sauce. Plenty to react to that has nothing to do with gluten.

Why real sourdough is different

This is the part that surprises people. Traditionally made sourdough is fermented slowly over many hours or days. That long fermentation predigests much of the starch and breaks down a large share of the fructans — the fermentable carbs that trigger bloating in a lot of "gluten-sensitive" people. Same grain, completely different effect on your gut. Many people who react to supermarket bread are perfectly fine with a real, naturally leavened sourdough.

What I see all the time

Someone spends years avoiding all bread, convinced it's gluten. We clean up the processed food and seed oils instead — and suddenly they can enjoy real, naturally made sourdough with zero issues. The bread was never the problem.

How to actually find your trigger

Instead of banning an entire food group forever, test it properly:

  • Clean up the obvious stuff first. Cut seed oils and ultra-processed food for a few weeks and see how much of the bloating disappears on its own.
  • Then reintroduce one thing at a time. Try real sourdough on its own — not as part of a processed meal — and notice how you actually feel.
  • Watch how you eat, not just what. Eating too fast, under stress, or in huge portions causes bloating that gets blamed on food. Slow down and see what changes.
  • Get the whole picture. If symptoms persist even with clean, whole food, that's worth exploring properly — with testing — rather than guessing.

Cutting gluten "just in case" can mean giving up real, nourishing food for a decade over a problem you never actually had. The goal isn't to fear bread — it's to find out what your body is really reacting to, and usually it's the industrial stuff, not the grain.
