The Steady Library · Gut

Fiber: the boring nutrient doing most of the work.

It gets none of the hype of protein or the fear of carbs, and yet most people eat about half of what they need. That gap quietly shows up as blood sugar swings, constant hunger, and a cranky gut. The fix is almost embarrassingly simple: eat more plants, on purpose.

The gap almost everyone lives in

The rough target for adults is somewhere around 25 to 35 grams of fiber a day. Most people get closer to 15. That's not a small miss — it's living permanently at half. And because nothing dramatic happens when you're low, nobody connects the daily fog, the 4pm hunger, or the sluggish gut back to a nutrient they never think about.

Fiber isn't one thing

There are two broad kinds, and you want both — but you don't need to track them. Soluble fiber (oats, beans, apples, chia) dissolves into a gel that slows digestion, blunts blood sugar, and feeds your gut bacteria. Insoluble fiber (vegetable skins, whole grains, nuts) adds bulk and keeps things moving. The good news: whole plant foods come with a mix of both, so "eat a variety of plants" handles the whole thing without a spreadsheet.

Why fiber steadies your blood sugar

This is the connection to nearly everything else on this site. That soluble gel physically slows how fast sugar from a meal enters your bloodstream — so the same carbs cause a gentler rise instead of a spike-and-crash. It's the same reason eating in order works: fiber first means a flatter curve, steadier energy, and fewer cravings later. Fiber is one of the most powerful, least-talked-about tools for the 3pm slump.

Fiber is your gut's actual food

Here's the part that changes how you think about it: fiber isn't really your food — it's food for the trillions of bacteria in your gut. When they ferment it, they produce short-chain fatty acids (like butyrate) that nourish the lining of your colon, calm inflammation, and keep the gut barrier strong. Starve them of fiber and the whole system gets less resilient. This is why the honest answer to "how do I improve my gut health" is usually "eat more plants," not "buy a probiotic."

The quiet appetite lever

Fiber-rich foods take up more room, hold water, and digest slowly, so they keep you full on fewer calories — without any willpower involved. If you're hungry an hour after eating, a low-fiber meal is often why. Add fiber and satiety mostly takes care of itself.

How to actually get more

The trick is to add, not subtract. You're not cutting anything — you're crowding your plate with plants.

  • Make beans and lentils a default. The single highest-fiber, cheapest move there is. A half-cup of lentils is around 8 grams — half your gap in one spoonful. Stir them into soups, salads, and sauces.
  • Add one vegetable to every meal. Not a whole salad — just one. Spinach into the eggs, a side of broccoli, peppers in the stir-fry. It compounds fast across a day.
  • Eat the fruit, skin and all. Whole fruit — berries, a pear, an apple — brings fiber that juice strips out entirely. Berries are the fiber-per-bite champion.
  • Start the day with oats or add seeds. A tablespoon of chia or ground flax (about 4–5g of fiber) disappears into oatmeal, yogurt, or a smoothie. Oats themselves are rich in the soluble kind.
  • Choose the less-processed version. Whole grains over refined, the potato with its skin, popcorn over chips. Same food, far more fiber — the theme of the whole library.
  • Go slow, and drink water. This one matters: ramp fiber up gradually over a couple of weeks and drink enough water, or you'll get gas and bloating. That discomfort is temporary and avoidable — it's a pacing problem, not a sign fiber doesn't agree with you.
Start here today

Add one thing to one meal: a scoop of beans, a handful of berries, or a tablespoon of chia. That's 4–8 grams toward your gap, from a single easy habit. Do it daily before you think about anything fancier.

One caveat: if you have IBS or a diagnosed gut condition, some high-fiber foods (certain beans, onions, and other FODMAPs) can genuinely worsen symptoms even though fiber is good in general. If more plants reliably make you feel worse rather than better, that's a real signal — work with someone rather than pushing through it.
